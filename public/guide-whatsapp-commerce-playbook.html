Playbook

Practical tactics for selling well on WhatsApp.

WhatsApp doesn't reward clever tricks. It rewards small, consistent habits — the same ones that make any shopkeeper good at their job, applied to a chat thread instead of a counter. Here's what actually moves the needle.

Good product photos are your storefront lighting

On a real street, lighting and window display do half the selling before a customer says a word. On WhatsApp, your product photo is that window. It doesn't need a studio — it needs to be in focus, well-lit (daylight near a window beats any indoor bulb), and show the actual item a customer will receive, not a stock image that sets the wrong expectation.

Shoot one item at a time against a plain background if you can. If you sell variants — sizes, colors, flavors — photograph each one separately rather than describing the difference in text. A customer scrolling a catalog decides in seconds; make the decision easy.

Reply fast, or set expectations

Speed is the single biggest lever you control. A customer who messages three shops and hears back from you first has usually already decided. WA-B answers the first message instantly with your greeting and menu, which buys you time — but once a real question comes in ("do you have this in blue?"), the clock is running again.

If you genuinely can't reply within a few minutes during busy hours, say so up front in your welcome message: "We reply within 30 minutes during lunch rush." An honest wait beats silence every time — silence reads as "this shop isn't paying attention."

Broadcast etiquette: earn the mute-free inbox

A broadcast list is a privilege, not a right. Every customer who lets you message them is one bad broadcast away from muting you — or opting out entirely. The shops that keep an engaged list follow a simple rule: message when it's useful to the customer, not just useful to you.

A restock alert on something they've bought before is useful. A weekly "check out our shop!" with no news in it is not. Keep the ratio heavily in favor of the former, and always make opting out easy and immediate — a frustrated customer who can't leave a list will complain publicly instead.

Try this. Before you hit send on a broadcast, ask: "would I want this message if I were the customer?" If the honest answer is no, don't send it yet.

Promos that don't feel like spam

The best-performing promos are usually the most specific ones. "10% off this weekend" competes with every other shop's generic discount. "Bring back this code for your next order of the same thing you bought last time" feels personal, because it is. Tie promos to something real — a slow Tuesday, a new batch that needs to move, a thank-you for a customer's third order — rather than running a discount on a loop.

Set an end date on every promo and actually enforce it. A "limited time" offer that never ends teaches customers to wait you out.

Handling delivery without dropping the thread

The moment an order leaves your hands, a customer's anxiety starts climbing — "did they forget me?" The fix isn't a phone call, it's a short automatic update at each real status change: preparing, on the way, delivered. It costs you nothing once it's set up, and it's the difference between a customer refreshing WhatsApp anxiously and one who trusts the process enough to order again.

If you use delivery zones, be upfront about fees before checkout, not after. Surprise delivery charges are one of the fastest ways to lose a cart at the last step.

Turning receipts into repeat orders

A receipt is the last thing most shops think about and the first thing a customer reads after paying. It's also free real estate. A warm, specific thank-you — "Thanks for the order, Ama! Same time next week?" — does more for repeat business than any promo, because it arrives at the exact moment the customer is already thinking well of you.

The shops that grow fastest on WhatsApp aren't running more promotions than everyone else. They're just consistently good at the fundamentals above — photos, speed, useful messages, honest delivery updates — applied every single day.
